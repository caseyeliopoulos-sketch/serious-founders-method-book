Foreword from the Faculty

The practitioner who arrives at this volume seeking shortcuts will find none. The étude, as a pedagogical form, has always demanded of the student a quality rarer than talent: sustained, methodical attention to what is actually in front of them. The composer of the étude does not introduce difficulties for their own sake. The difficulties are the lesson. The student who wishes to work around the technical problem rather than through it is not studying; they are pretending to study, which is a considerably more expensive use of one's time.

The ten études collected here address the fundamental technical problems that confront every practitioner of entrepreneurial craft, regardless of instrument or register. They are arranged in order of grade, from elementary to advanced — the student will find, upon completion of the final study, that the first has become newly comprehensible. This is as it should be. The faculty has drawn upon the lectures and public testimonies of distinguished practitioners across many seasons, and has endeavored to distill from this material only those observations that have withstood the scrutiny of repeated application in conditions of genuine uncertainty.

The tempo markings throughout this volume are not suggestions. They are technical specifications. The student who plays the premature-scaling étude at allegro rather than lento is not demonstrating capability; they are demonstrating that they have not read the marking. All faculty notes are to be read before the passage is attempted, not after.

The practice checklists at the conclusion of each étude are mandatory. A box that remains unchecked is a skill that remains undeveloped. The faculty does not accept incomplete assignments.

— The Faculty, Conservatory of Applied Product Science

Op. 1, No. 1 · First Position: The Customer Inquiry 7

Étude I · Opus 1, No. 1 · Grade I

First Position: The Customer Inquiry

A Foundational Study in User Research for the Beginning Practitioner

Adagio interrogativo — con attenzione completa alla risposta
Slowly, with an inquiring disposition — with complete attention to the answer
Grade I · Elementary

Dedicated to the Ninety Who Said No, Without Whom the Ten Who Said Yes Could Not Have Been Found

The staff is empty for the same reason companies fail. Alströmer notes the cause is quite simple. The faculty declines to repeat it.

Gustaf Alstromer, a Group Partner at Y Combinator who has observed several hundred early-stage companies in the years since building Airbnb's original growth team, has identified the root cause of startup failure with a directness the faculty finds both instructive and, in most cases, uncomfortable to receive: companies fail because they do not talk to customers. Not because they build the wrong thing — though they do — but because the remedy for building the wrong thing requires the prior activity of talking to customers, which has not been performed. The Y Combinator curriculum orients every incoming company, from its first session, toward a single recurring question: what did you learn from talking to customers this week? Not what did you build. Not what did you raise. What did you learn. The student who finds this question easy to answer has an advantage that no quantity of technical sophistication can replicate. The student who finds the question difficult has identified, before any other analysis is required, their most consequential technical deficit.

The practitioner who understands the principle but does not execute it has been defeated by one of two obstacles. The first is fear — specifically, the fear of rejection, which the student has misread as evidence of the product's inadequacy. Alstromer's corrective is precise: the person who signs up and does not return is not your critic. They are indifferent. They had a full life before your product existed and they continue to have a full life now. They were not your early adopter, and their departure cost you nothing, which is approximately what their engagement would also have cost you. The ten percent of any market who constitute genuine early adopters — the practitioners who will remain on the telephone with you, engage with an imperfect prototype, and articulate what actually matters — require, by the mathematics of their rarity, that you contact one hundred people in order to find ten, and from those ten extract the insight that would otherwise cost you a year of misdirected engineering. Alstromer's documented minimum for a viable research base is twenty-five to fifty conversations. Students who believe five will suffice have encountered the second obstacle, which is arithmetic overconfidence and is not meaningfully different from the first.

The faculty's preferred method for these conversations proceeds in a sequence the Y Combinator curriculum has established through extensive trial. What software is the customer currently using to address this problem? What are they paying for it? What problems does their current solution fail to address? How intensely do they feel those problems? These questions are arranged in ascending order of diagnostic value, and the final question — the intensity of the felt problem — is the one most frequently omitted and the one that most directly predicts whether anyone will pay for a solution. The practitioner should listen. The practitioner should not pitch. The student who pitches during a discovery conversation has mistaken the audition for the concert, and the customer — who had no intention of attending a concert — will exit without comment, because the comment is not the kind they are prepared to make to a person they have just met. The Airbnb founders, upon being asked by Paul Graham why they were sitting in their office rather than in New York talking to their hosts, departed for New York. What they learned there — what was broken, what hosts needed, what the product had not yet understood — is documented. What would have happened had they not gone is also, in a sense, documented: it would have looked like every other company that remained in the office.

Faculty Note

Gustaf Alstromer is a Group Partner at Y Combinator and former lead of Airbnb's original growth team. His identification of talking to customers as the single root-cause failure pattern — "if I drill down what makes companies fail, it's quite simple: they don't talk to users, which means they don't find product-market fit, and if they don't find product-market fit, nothing else really matters" — is reproduced from the conservatory lecture with the original language preserved. His observation regarding the early-adopter distribution (approximately ten percent of any given market) and the practical volume required (reaching out to one hundred contacts to secure twenty-five to fifty substantive conversations) constitutes the quantitative foundation of this étude's practice assignment. The Paul Graham anecdote regarding Airbnb is a matter of YC institutional record, confirmed by multiple participants including Alstromer himself.

Primary source: G. Alstromer, "Gustaf Alstromer on YC, growth, and the most common startup mistakes," Lenny's Podcast, 2 March 2023.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Contact twenty potential users this week. Do not pitch. Ask only: "What is the hardest part of [the problem your product addresses]?" Write down the exact words each person uses — not your interpretation of what they meant, but the words themselves.
On your next discovery call, set a timer. If you have spoken for more than thirty percent of the call's elapsed time, the call was a pitch, not a discovery session. Schedule a replacement call and do not speak first.
Read your product's current homepage copy. Underline every phrase that originated in a direct customer conversation. Count the underlined phrases and the unmarked ones. This ratio is a measurement. Improve it before the next session.
Op. 1, No. 2 · Allegro for the Unautomated Task 17

Étude II · Opus 1, No. 2 · Grade II

Allegro for the Unautomated Task

On the Virtue of Playing by Hand Before Engaging the Mechanism

Allegro grazioso — ma rigorosamente a mano
Fairly quickly — but strictly by hand
Grade II · Elementary Difficulty

Dedicated to Joe Gebbia, Who Was Given Permission

This passage is to be completed by hand. No mechanism has been approved for this stage. Prohovnik notes this is not a limitation. It is the instrument.

The practitioner who reaches for the mechanism before the melody has been established by hand has committed a technical error that no subsequent sophistication of the mechanism will correct. The mechanism, once engaged, will execute with great efficiency whatever it has been configured to execute. If it has been configured to deliver something that the customer does not in fact want, it will deliver that thing at scale, with increasing confidence, until the practitioner notices — which is typically later than the customer did. The faculty has observed no case in which automating an unvalidated process produced a validated outcome. The direction of causality is irreversible: the hand first, the mechanism second. This is not a philosophical preference. It is a sequence constraint.

Joe Gebbia, co-founder of Airbnb, has described the moment at which this permission was granted to him with a clarity that the faculty considers irreproducible in paraphrase: "It wasn't until our first session with Paul Graham at Y Combinator where we basically — the first time someone gave us permission to do things that don't scale, and it was in that moment, and I'll never forget it because it changed the trajectory of the business." The faculty notes the vocabulary of permission. The aspiring practitioner typically understands, at some level, that direct, manual, unscalable action is available to them. What they require is permission — which is to say, absolution from the expectation that every action must be designed for infinite repetition before it is attempted once. This absolution is freely granted by the faculty. The student may proceed.

Crystal Widjaja, who led growth at Gojek at enormous scale and has advised many early-stage companies since, has offered a quantitative frame for the value of manual execution that the faculty finds clarifying: every idea is cheap at thirty people. The student should therefore spend their most productive early hours not in the automation of process but in the direct, manual, and effortful execution of the thing they eventually intend to automate — because it is only through this manual execution that the thing worth automating can be identified. The question that automation answers is "how do I do this faster?" The question that manual execution answers is "is this the right thing to do?" The second question must precede the first. Lenny Rachitsky has offered the faculty's preferred formulation of the correct sequencing: "Do things that don't scale, and then scale the things that you're doing." The order of operations is non-negotiable. The student who reverses it will find themselves in possession of a very efficient mechanism for delivering something that required, above all, reconsideration.

Faculty Note

The principle addressed in this étude has been independently confirmed by guest faculty across many sessions: Keith Yandell (DoorDash) has noted that doing things that don't scale "is as relevant today as it was then"; Brian Tolkin (Opendoor, Uber) endorsed the faculty's preferred formulation during a conservatory lecture; Gustaf Alströmer (Y Combinator) has identified it as among the most reliably generative pieces of advice the organization provides to incoming companies. The Gebbia quotation was preserved verbatim from Lenny's Newsletter's account of Airbnb's early history, where it appears in the context of the company's now-famous practice of personally photographing host apartments in New York — an act that was entirely manual, entirely unscalable, and entirely responsible for the product's early traction.

Primary sources: L. Rachitsky, "How to Kickstart and Scale a Marketplace Business — Part 4," Lenny's Newsletter, 3 December 2019; "Lessons from scaling Uber and Opendoor," Lenny's Podcast, episode featuring Brian Tolkin, 4 August 2024; "How to scrappily hire for, measure, and unlock growth," Lenny's Podcast, episode featuring Crystal Widjaja, 31 July 2022.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Identify one process that has been automated in the last six months. Verify that the manual version of this process was performed enough times to confirm it was worth automating.
Identify one thing your product does for users that you have never done manually yourself, on behalf of a real user, in real time. Do it this week.
List the three activities currently delegated to software or systems that, if performed by a human, would reveal something the system currently cannot tell you. Arrange to have a human perform at least one of them.
Op. 1, No. 3 · Variations on a Value Metric 27

Étude III · Opus 1, No. 3 · Grade IV

Variations on a Value Metric

A Study in Monetization Fundamentals for the Intermediate Practitioner

Con precisione — e riveduto trimestralmente
With precision — and revised each quarter
Grade IV · Foundational

Dedicated to the Area Under the Demand Curve, Long Neglected

The faculty notes that the practitioner has likely done nothing with these lines. Campbell suggests revisiting them once a quarter. That is all.

The practitioner who approaches pricing as a single decision — a number, selected, and thereafter largely undisturbed — has misconceived the nature of the exercise. Pricing is a phrase, not a note. It encompasses the segment being addressed, the packaging in which the product is offered, the value proposition being communicated, and the unit of measurement being applied to the exchange. Of these elements, the unit of measurement — the value metric — is the most consequential. Patrick Campbell of ProfitWell, who has studied this problem across thousands of subscription companies, has stated the foundational principle directly: "If you get everything else wrong in pricing, but you get your value metric right, you'll do OK." This is not hyperbole. The faculty has confirmed it through sustained observation.

The value metric is what you charge for. It ought to correspond as closely as possible to the value the customer actually receives: the number of contacts managed, the revenue recovered on their behalf, the connections enabled, the time saved. The practitioner who charges a flat monthly fee regardless of customer size is, in the language of economics, leaving the area under the demand curve unaddressed — a region of revenue that belongs, by right, to the provider of value, and which is surrendered through a failure of measurement. The company that finds its value metric and prices against it will, according to documented evidence, grow at double the rate with half the churn and twice the expansion revenue of a company charging a flat fee for equivalent value delivered. The difference is not in the product. It is in the accounting of what the product does.

The faculty issues three further technical instructions, each derived from empirical observation rather than theoretical reasoning. First: do not discount beyond twenty percent. The data demonstrates that large discounts attract customers who then depart, having received the reduction without committing to the relationship. The correlation between discount size and subsequent churn is, the faculty regrets to report, nearly perfect. Second: treat your pricing as a living practice. The most successful companies adjust some aspect of their monetization every quarter — not necessarily the price itself, but the packaging, the proposition, or the segmentation. The companies that do this consistently grow their revenue per customer at a rate that companies with static pricing do not. Third: freemium is an acquisition strategy, not a pricing tier. The practitioner who deploys freemium before they understand how to convert a lead to a customer will produce a larger and more confused pipeline, which is not an improvement on a smaller and clearer one.

Faculty Note

This étude is drawn primarily from the work of Patrick Campbell, CEO of ProfitWell, who served as guest faculty for the conservatory's session on monetization. Campbell's quantitative research on SaaS pricing — encompassing thousands of subscription companies — is among the most rigorous available. Notable findings reproduced in this étude include: value-metric pricing companies growing at 2× the rate with ½ the churn; discounts above 20% correlating with higher churn; quarterly experimentation correlating with improving revenue per customer; and design quality boosting willingness to pay by approximately 20% in contemporary product contexts.

Primary source: Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell), "Pricing your SaaS product," Lenny's Newsletter, 27 October 2020.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Write your current value metric in one sentence. If you cannot, your pricing foundation requires attention before any other optimization is attempted.
Review your last five deals that were discounted. Note the current retention status of each customer acquired at a discount above twenty percent.
Identify one aspect of your monetization to experiment with in the next ninety days. It need not be the price. It may be the packaging, the positioning, or the value proposition. The requirement is simply that something be tested.

Étude IV · Opus 1, No. 4 · Grade V

The Exercise of Competitive Alternatives

On Positioning and the Problem of Being Inaudible

Chiaramente — senza equivoci
Clearly — without ambiguity of any kind
Grade V · Moderate Difficulty

Dedicated to the Second Sales Call, Which Should Not Have Been Necessary

The staff is empty because the practitioner has not yet decided what they are. Dunford observes that forty percent of lost deals were lost here, before the performance began.

The practitioner who lacks a clear position is not modest. They are inaudible. April Dunford, who has worked with more than two hundred companies on the specific technical problem of positioning — including organizations of the stature of Google, IBM, and Postman — defines the requirement with economical precision: positioning establishes how your product is the best in the world at delivering some value that a well-defined set of companies care a great deal about. Each element of this definition is load-bearing. The word "best" requires that the product be superior to alternatives in a specific, demonstrable way. The phrase "well-defined set" requires that the target be named, not gestured toward. The phrase "care a great deal about" requires evidence, not assumption.

The diagnostic for weak positioning is the additional sales call. When a customer requires a second meeting to understand what they heard in the first, the positioning has failed. The practitioner may further identify this condition by accompanying the sales team on calls and listening for the customer who says, "Back up and pitch it to me again" — or, more damaging still, "I can do this on a spreadsheet; why do I need you?" Dunford notes that in B2B markets, approximately forty percent of lost deals are lost not to a named competitor but to inaction: to the spreadsheet, to the intern, to the status quo. The practitioner who is not explicitly positioning against the status quo is conceding forty percent of their market without contest, which the faculty considers an unacceptable technical error.

The five-component positioning exercise proceeds as follows. First, name the competitive alternatives — every option available to the customer who does not choose your product, including doing nothing. Second, identify the unique attributes of your product relative to those alternatives. Third, translate those attributes into differentiated value — what the customer can do, earn, or avoid that they could not without you. Fourth, identify the characteristics of customers who care specifically about that differentiated value, which is the definition of your target segment. Fifth, name the market context in which your value is most legible to the people you are going after. Dunford's experience is that this exercise takes one week and produces more clarity than any quantity of branding discussion. The faculty has found no credible reason to dispute this estimate.

Caution to the Student The faculty notes that positioning problems are frequently misidentified as product problems, messaging problems, or sales execution problems. The root cause is almost always misalignment between what the founding team believes they are communicating and what the customer is actually receiving. Attend to the source, not the symptoms.
Faculty Note

This étude draws primarily on the work of April Dunford, author of Obviously Awesome and the world's foremost practitioner of B2B product positioning. Dunford's observation that most positioning problems are, in fact, misalignment problems within the founding team deserves particular emphasis: the founder often holds the correct positioning in their head, having developed it through direct customer conversations in the earliest days of the company, while the broader leadership team has drifted from that original precision. Dunford's five-step framework has been applied successfully at organizations from Help Scout to Magic Leap; the faculty recommends it without qualification to any practitioner who cannot answer the question "what are we the best option for, and for whom?" in thirty seconds.

Primary source: April Dunford, "Product positioning, segmentation, and optimizing your sales process," summarized in Lenny's Newsletter, 13 August 2024; episode originally published at Lenny's Podcast.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Name every competitive alternative to your product, including spreadsheets, manual processes, and doing nothing. If the list surprises you, your positioning exercise is overdue.
Accompany a sales call this week. Count the number of times the customer asks for clarification about what the product does or who it is for. Each instance is a positioning measurement.
Ask five people at your company — from different functions — to describe your positioning in one sentence. Compare the answers. The degree of divergence is a direct measurement of the problem.

Étude V · Opus 1, No. 5 · Grade V

On the Sustaining of the User

A Study in Cohort Retention for the Intermediate Practitioner

Tenuto assai — con molta pazienza
Sustained at considerable length — with great patience
Grade V · Moderate Difficulty

Dedicated to the Six-Month Cohort

Whether this staff is revisited is within the practitioner's control. Campbell notes that twenty-five to forty percent of the problem is visible here. The student has been looking elsewhere.

The first principle the serious practitioner must internalize is this: retention is the instrument through which all other expression flows. Acquisition without retention is not a growth strategy; it is a performance of growth, which is a different and considerably more expensive thing. The retention curve is the only honest document your business produces. It cannot be glossed, positioned, or reframed. It either flattens or it does not. When it flattens — when a cohort of users reaches a stable floor and remains — the practitioner has found evidence of genuine value. When it continues its descent toward zero, the practitioner has found evidence of something else, and should attend to it without delay.

The faculty has assembled, through consultation with twenty of the most experienced growth practitioners then working, a set of concrete benchmarks for what constitutes adequate and excellent retention at the six-month interval. These are not aspirational targets. They are the minimum threshold of competence expected at this conservatory. Consumer social practitioners are expected to sustain no fewer than twenty-five percent of their original cohort; excellent practitioners sustain forty-five. Enterprise practitioners are held to a higher standard, with the adequate threshold at seventy percent and excellence beginning at ninety. The student who finds their numbers below the adequate line is not failed — they are redirected, which is a form of teaching.

Register Adequate (Good) Excellent (Great)
Consumer Social~25%~45%
Consumer Transactional~30%~50%
Consumer SaaS~40%~70%
SMB / Mid-Market SaaS~60%~80%
Enterprise SaaS~70%~90%

Seven technical corrections are available to the practitioner whose retention falls below the adequate line. They are presented here in order of expected impact, not order of ease. The first correction is to improve the product — to solve the customer's problem more completely, more reliably, or at lower cost. This correction is the most fundamental and the most difficult. The second correction is to improve onboarding, which is to say: to ensure that the value already present in the instrument is reliably communicated to the new student. Andrew Chen, former head of growth at Uber and now a partner at Andreessen Horowitz, has offered the observation that most product teams attend to the wrong end of this problem: they add features for customers who are already retained, when the actual technical leverage lies entirely in the experience of those who are encountering the instrument for the first time. The faculty endorses this observation without reservation.

The remaining five corrections — making the product stickier, catching departing users before they have fully exited, reminding users of the value they are receiving, retrieving users who have already departed, and changing the composition of the user base itself — are presented not in diminishing order of importance but in diminishing order of glamour. The final correction, adjusting the quality and intent of users being acquired, requires no technical virtuosity. It requires only the honesty to acknowledge that paid acquisition traffic behaves differently from organic traffic, and that optimizing for a retention metric while simultaneously degrading the quality of the cohort is a form of arithmetic self-deception.

Caution to the Student The faculty notes the frequent error of consulting long-term retained customers when seeking to improve retention. This approach produces features appreciated by the converted and irrelevant to the unconverted. The new-user experience is the technical site of the problem. Attend to it there.
Faculty Note

The benchmarks contained in this étude were established through original research conducted by the conservatory in consultation with twenty distinguished growth practitioners, including Casey Winters (Pinterest, Grubhub), Brian Balfour (Reforge), Elena Verna (SurveyMonkey), and Andrew Chen (a16z), among others. Casey Winters has described high retention as "growth's equivalent of the triple word score." Brian Balfour has offered the following, which the faculty commends to every student regardless of their current retention figure: "If you have poor retention, nothing else matters."

Primary source: L. Rachitsky, "What is good retention?" and "How to increase your product's retention," Lenny's Newsletter, 9 June 2020 and 25 August 2020.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Construct a cohort retention chart for each of the last six user cohorts and observe whether the curve flattens.
Identify which of the seven technical corrections your product has not yet attempted. Begin with the one ranked highest on the list, not the one that seems easiest.
Speak directly with one user who departed in the last thirty days. Ask only why. Do not explain.

Étude VI · Opus 1, No. 6 · Grade V

Modulation to a New Key

On the Controlled Change of Direction, and How to Know Whether One Is Warranted

Sospeso — poi, con decisione irrevocabile
Suspended — then, with irrevocable resolve
Grade V · Advanced Difficulty

Dedicated to the Game That Was Shut Down, to the Chat Feature That Survived It, and to the Three Years of Unglamorous Work That Separated the Two

The previous melody has been set aside. Systrom knew it was time when people kept bouncing off. Rachitsky offers a simpler test: if the gut says pivot, it is probably time.

The practitioner who has decided to pivot and the practitioner who is attempting to determine whether a pivot is warranted are engaged in activities that share a name and almost nothing else. The faculty therefore opens this étude with a taxonomic distinction upon which all subsequent instruction depends. There are two distinct categories of directional change. The first is the ideation pivot, available to an early-stage company within approximately three months of its founding, before a fully formed product exists or meaningful traction has accumulated. Brex moved from virtual reality hardware to business banking; Retool from a British peer-to-peer payments application to a platform for building internal tools; YouTube from a video-based dating site to a general video platform — in each case within three months, in some cases within a week. These changes involved near-total abandonment of the original direction and required principally the courage to stop. The second category is the hard pivot — undertaken by a company with live users and real data, retaining one element of the prior direction and committing entirely to it. Instagram stripped away its location check-in application and retained only the photo-sharing and filter feature. Slack retained the internal communication tool its team had built to coordinate work on a game, after shutting down the game on which forty-five people had labored for three and a half years. The hard pivot keeps the stone the sculptor chose not to chip away. Both categories require courage. Only the second requires that something be present worth keeping — and the identification of that something requires the habits of observation that this conservatory has described, in Étude I, as the foundational technique.

The signals that a pivot is warranted have been documented across enough companies with sufficient consistency that the faculty presents them here not as opinions but as measurements. The first signal is persistent lukewarm interest: a retention curve that descends without flattening; a growth plateau without an identifiable ceiling; a response from users that reads as mild curiosity rather than urgent need. Kevin Systrom on Instagram's predecessor: "We knew it wasn't working when we would give it to people and they'd just keep bouncing off." Shahed Khan on Loom, seven months into its prior direction: "We only made $600." Jack Altman on Lattice's original OKR product: "The user retention was really bad, and after a few months there was a massive drop-off in how many employees were using the tool." The second signal is the revision of a market-size conviction — not a projection adjustment, but a fundamental change in what the founder believes the idea can become. Stewart Butterfield on Glitch, the game that became Slack: "It might have been a neat project for a half-dozen people if we had spent a million dollars to get there, but by the end of 2012, there were 45 people working on it, we had spent many millions of dollars, and it just wasn't ever going to scale." When either signal has persisted for two to three months in the ideation phase, or six or more months with a live product, four strategies for finding the next idea are available. One: identify a single feature currently showing disproportionate pull, and commit to it entirely. This is how Instagram, Loom, Yelp, Pinterest, and Flickr each found their direction. Two: identify an internal tool the team built for itself that others want — this is how Slack, Segment, Amplitude, Shopify, and Discord each found theirs. Three: attend to an adjacent market larger than the one currently being addressed, as Box, Twitch, and Coinbase each did. Four: generate new ideas from scratch through deliberate ideation — which is the path that produced Twitter, Lyft, and Brex. Three of the four strategies require only that the practitioner pay sustained attention to what is already happening. The faculty considers this an unusually accessible approach to a decision of such consequence.

The decision to persist rather than pivot has been reduced, by Scott Belsky — Chief Strategy Officer at Adobe and a guest of this conservatory — to a single diagnostic question, which the faculty reproduces here as the most reliable instrument available for this measurement: how much conviction do you have in the solution you are building, knowing everything you now know? Founders who answer "more than when I started — I have learned things that have increased my conviction" are, in Belsky's formulation, in the messy middle, which is distinguishable from failure only by what they do next. Founders who answer "if I had known then what I know now, I would not have started this" are advised, with care and without equivocation, to stop. Dalton Caldwell, Managing Director at Y Combinator, has provided a formalization of this judgment that the faculty reproduces without modification: if the quotient of how well things are working divided by the number of months of full-time effort is less than the product of one's excitement to pursue a different idea multiplied by one's confidence that a better idea can be found, the student should pivot. The faculty notes, in closing, that the majority of startups which ultimately fail will have attempted at least one pivot before doing so. This is not an argument against pivoting. It is an argument against the belief that pivoting is itself the solution — and an argument for returning, after the pivot, to the discipline described in the first page of this volume, which has always been the same discipline and has always preceded everything else.

The case studies in this étude — Brex, Retool, YouTube, Instagram, Slack, Loom, Lattice, Yelp, Pinterest, Flickr, Segment, Amplitude, Shopify, Discord, Twitter, Lyft, Coinbase, Twitch, and Box — were drawn from Lenny Rachitsky's research into forty documented startup pivots, described by the conservatory as the largest such dataset assembled. The Systrom, Khan, Altman, and Butterfield quotations are reproduced from the original primary sources cited in that research. Scott Belsky (CSO, Adobe) is quoted from his Lenny's Podcast appearance. Dalton Caldwell's equation is reproduced from his YC talk on pivoting, which Rachitsky has described as "legendary" and which the faculty has found, upon review, to be accurately described. The observation that most failed startups will have attempted a pivot — drawn from a poll of former founders conducted across LinkedIn and X — is noted here as a cautionary statistic, not a recommendation against the practice.

Primary source: L. Rachitsky, "The art of the pivot, part 2: How, why, and when to pivot," Lenny's Newsletter, 14 May 2024.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Determine in writing whether your current situation is an ideation pivot context (under three months, no meaningful traction) or a hard pivot context (live product, real users). The strategy appropriate to one is not appropriate to the other, and conflating them produces a third category of error the faculty has not found to be useful.
Answer Scott Belsky's question in writing, alone, before discussing it with your co-founder: knowing everything you now know, do you have more or less conviction in the solution you are building than you had when you began? Do not revise the written answer after the conversation.
List every feature in your product sorted by frequency of use. Identify whether the most-used feature is the one you designed the product to deliver. If it is not, you have found the question this étude exists to prompt.
Op. 1, No. 7 · The Prelude to the First Movement 67

Étude VII · Opus 1, No. 7 · Grade VI

The Prelude to the First Movement

On Product-Market Fit and the Patience Required to Find It

Senza fretta — con intenzione rigorosa
Without hurry — with rigorous intention
Grade VI · Considerable Difficulty

Dedicated to the Pivot, Before It Became Necessary

The faculty has provided the staff. The student will know when the market has written on it. Rachleff suggests pulling the trial to find out. If no one screams, the staff remains blank.

The student who mistakes the tuning of the instrument for the performance itself has committed an error of sequence, which is the most consequential category of error available to the early-stage practitioner. The instrument must first be tuned — which is to say, product-market fit must be identified and confirmed — before the concert hall is staffed, the marketing budget is distributed, and the performance is announced to the public. The practitioner who reverses this sequence will produce, at considerable expense, a very loud and very well-attended demonstration of an instrument that is not yet in tune.

The faculty has observed that product-market fit presents itself across four distinct registers: nascent, developing, strong, and extreme. The student will wish to know that approximately sixty percent of practicing startups will never advance beyond the developing register. This statistic is offered not as discouragement but as a calibration. The student who locates themselves accurately within this taxonomy — neither inflating their register out of optimism nor deflating it out of false modesty — is already in possession of information that the majority of their cohort lacks. Correct self-diagnosis is a prerequisite for correct treatment.

In general, hiring before you get product-market fit slows you down, and hiring after you get product-market fit speeds you up. Until you get product-market fit, you want to: live as long as possible, and iterate as quickly as possible. — Sam Altman, as cited in Lenny's Newsletter

The diagnostic for genuine product-market fit is neither a survey nor a Net Promoter Score. It is the behavior of the retention curve. When the curve flattens, when users return without prompting, when the loss of the product would cause genuine disruption rather than mild inconvenience — the practitioner has found what they were looking for. Todd Jackson of First Round Capital, who has studied this problem with unusual rigor, identifies three further dimensions by which a healthy fit may be recognized: the demand for the product is clear and demonstrable; the product satisfies that demand better than available alternatives; and the efficiency of delivery is sufficient to sustain the enterprise. The practitioner who can affirm all three has located the starting line. What comes before is preparation. What comes after is the race.

A final technical note: the faculty does not recommend excessive debate regarding whether product-market fit has been achieved. The signal, when genuine, is difficult to misread. It arrives less as a conclusion reached through deliberation than as a condition noticed through accumulation — a growing number of users who came back, who told others, who objected when features were removed. When these signals are absent, additional deliberation will not produce them. Additional practice will.

Faculty Note

The four-level taxonomy of product-market fit (nascent, developing, strong, extreme) was developed by Todd Jackson and the First Round Capital team through analysis of hundreds of early-stage companies. Jackson has observed that "finding product-market fit is the single most important thing that your startup does in the first three years, and it's just underexplored and underexplained as a topic." The Sam Altman quotation above has been reproduced in the faculty's notes from multiple lectures and is considered foundational to the conservatory's curriculum. The "premature scaling" formulation is attributed to Steve Blank and was cited by Andreessen Horowitz in published materials reviewed by the faculty.

Primary sources: L. Rachitsky, "When should a SaaS startup raise money," Lenny's Newsletter, 25 February 2020; and "A framework for finding product-market fit," Lenny's Podcast, episode featuring Todd Jackson, First Round Capital, 11 April 2024.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Place yourself honestly on the four-level taxonomy: nascent, developing, strong, or extreme. Write your answer down. Return to it in ninety days without looking at it first.
Identify the behavior — not the stated opinion — that would constitute the clearest evidence of fit in your specific register. Look for it.
Calculate how many days of runway remain at the current burn rate. Divide the answer into two categories: days available for finding fit, and days available for accelerating after fit is found.
Op. 1, No. 8 · The Assembly of the Chamber Ensemble 77

Étude VIII · Opus 1, No. 8 · Grade VII

The Assembly of the Chamber Ensemble

A Technical Study in Early-Stage Hiring for the Serious Founder

Con gravità e cura straordinaria
With gravity and extraordinary care
Grade VII · High Difficulty

Dedicated to the Chair That Remained Empty Until the Right Musician Was Found

The faculty has left these lines open. Corcos estimates one hundred hours to fill them correctly. Handel advises against filling them quickly.

The first ensemble is not assembled through advertisements. It is assembled through the clarity and ambition of the mission as articulated by the founder. Kevin Tan of Snackpass has described the mechanism with the precision of a principal player who has solved the technical problem: "Articulating a crystal clear and ambitious vision makes it easy for candidates to visualize what they are building towards for the next five years." The candidate who cannot, after a conversation with the founder, construct a credible picture of what the next five years might contain will decline, and correctly so. The vision is not a recruitment device. It is the instrument itself. The recruitment is the natural consequence of a candidate encountering an instrument they wish to play.

The composition of the early ensemble creates a dynamic of compounding consequence. First-rate musicians attract other first-rate musicians; the bar, once set at the correct height, tends to remain there through the internal demands of those who have already cleared it. Nick Handel of Transform has provided the technical caution that bears reproduction here without abbreviation: "Never lower your recruiting bar to hire someone because they can fill a short-term need. It will be very tempting to bring someone on if they are in front of you and ready to go, but it will have longer-term negative consequences if they are not the right person." The vacant chair is preferable to the misplaced musician. A vacant chair creates silence, which is uncomfortable. A misplaced musician creates dissonance, which is considerably worse, and is considerably harder to correct after the fact.

The practitioner who wishes to understand the actual work required to build a great early ensemble should attend carefully to the quantification provided by Sam Corcos of Levels: approximately one hundred hours per hire. The faculty neither endorses nor contests this figure; the faculty reproduces it because students routinely underestimate the investment required by a factor of eight to ten. The student who has spent twelve hours searching for a candidate and finds themselves without one should understand that eighty-eight hours remain in the exercise, not that the suitable candidate does not exist. Further: the best candidates are rarely actively seeking positions. They are encountered through a persistent, unhurried, and methodical cultivation of relationship. Siqi Chen of Runway has observed that their best hires were people who were not looking at the time of first contact — and who were slowly won over the course of approximately six months. The closing of a great hire resembles, in its structure, the closing of a great investor: it is a process of conviction, not a transaction.

Faculty Note

This étude draws on the direct testimonies of seven early-stage founders assembled and curated by the conservatory. Five consistent themes emerged from this research: the importance of a captivating vision, the compounding effect of a high-quality early team, the sheer volume of work required, the leverage of the founder's existing professional network, and the sophistication of the closing process. The conservatory is indebted to Brandon Hill (Vori), Julianna Lamb and Reed McGinley-Stempel (Stytch), Kevin Tan (Snackpass), Nick Handel (Transform), Sam Corcos (Levels), Siqi Chen (Runway), and Alex Bilmes (Endgame) for their contributions to this curriculum.

Primary source: L. Rachitsky, "Winning at early-stage hiring," Lenny's Newsletter, 6 July 2021.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Recite your company's mission to someone who has no professional stake in it. Note the moment at which their attention visibly shifts.
For your next open role, identify three people in your existing network who are not actively looking. Make contact this week without mentioning the open role.
Track the number of hours spent on your most recent hire. If it is fewer than sixty, the faculty respectfully suggests that more time remains in the exercise.
Op. 1, No. 9 · The Concert Hall Approach 87

Étude IX · Opus 1, No. 9 · Grade VII

The Concert Hall Approach

On Bringing the Performance to Its Audience — A Study in Revenue Motion for the Advanced Practitioner

Con preparazione meticolosa — poi, davanti al pubblico, con decisione
With meticulous preparation — then, before the audience, with resolve
Grade VII · High Difficulty

Dedicated to the Prospect Who Was Sold the Art of the Possible, and Who Purchased, Instead, the Status Quo

The conservatory's instruction concludes here. Grosser notes that eighty percent of customers buy to avoid pain. The practitioner is advised to write accordingly.

Go-to-market is not, as the student may have inferred from its colloquial deployment, a synonym for marketing. Jeanne DeWitt Grosser, who built Stripe's original sales organization from its first hire and serves as Chief Operating Officer at Vercel, has defined it with a precision the faculty endorses: any function that touches a customer or makes a dollar. This encompasses marketing, sales, customer success, field engineering, technical sales, support, and partnerships. The practitioner who treats these as separate concerns with separate strategies — each pursuing a slightly different customer through a slightly different channel with slightly different messaging toward a slightly different definition of success — will produce, with considerable efficiency, an organization in which the left hand does not know what the right hand is playing. The integrated management of these functions around a single customer lifecycle — from the first moment of awareness through high-LTV, multi-year renewal — is the structural definition of a mature go-to-market operation, and its absence is the structural definition of a premature one. The student should also internalize, before proceeding further, a single finding from Grosser's career that contradicts the instinct of most founders: approximately eighty percent of customers buy in order to avoid pain or reduce risk, not to capture upside. The founder who constructs their go-to-market narrative entirely around the art of the possible — around future capabilities, transformative vision, the horizon of everything the product will eventually enable — is addressing the twenty percent while systematically conceding the eighty. This is not a positioning error. It is an arithmetic one, and it does not respond to better messaging.

The correct sequence of go-to-market development begins with the founder as the primary revenue instrument, and this is not a transitional phase to be apologized for or accelerated past. It is a technical requirement with a specific duration that is determined not by a calendar but by a condition. The founder wins early customers through conviction, through the authenticity of having built the thing and understanding precisely what it does, and through the willingness to explain it with patience across an indefinite number of conversations. This is work that cannot be delegated before the condition has been met. Grosser's diagnostic for the condition is precise: if a company approaches one million dollars in annual recurring revenue and the customers it has acquired resemble no recognizable pattern — different industries, different sizes, different buying contexts, different problems being solved — it is still in the evangelist-sale phase, which does not scale through headcount. The transition to a hired sales function becomes appropriate only when the founder can write one sentence describing the customer the product fits: the industry, the company size, the role of the buyer, and the specific pain the purchase decision is driven by. Grosser's example: "We are good — our product fits with startups with fewer than a hundred employees who are typically building SaaS applications." Until that sentence can be written from evidence rather than aspiration, the first sales hire is not a growth lever. It is an expensive attempt to delegate an understanding that has not yet been developed.

The most transferable framework Grosser has offered — and the one the faculty has found most consistently applicable across stage and industry — is the instruction to treat the go-to-market motion as a product. A product team that ships without measurement is not running a product; it is conducting an uncontrolled experiment and calling the results a roadmap. A sales organization deployed into the field without documented discovery questions, without structured methods for handling objections, without instrumentation of where prospects disengage — is engaged in the same activity under a different name. The discipline of writing down what works, measuring each transition in the pipeline, and attending first and always to the stage where the highest percentage of customers exit, is identical in structure to the discipline of building software. The practitioner who has internalized this étude will recognize that the question "at what stage of the sales process do we lose the most qualified prospects?" is structurally equivalent to the question "at what step of the onboarding flow do the most new users disengage?" Both describe a process that can be redesigned. Both require the data to exist before they can be answered honestly. And both are answered through the same method: speaking to the people who left and asking, with the complete attention prescribed in Étude I of this volume, what caused them to go.

Faculty Note

Jeanne DeWitt Grosser is Chief Operating Officer at Vercel, overseeing marketing, sales, customer success, revenue operations, and field engineering. Prior to Vercel, she served as Chief Revenue Officer at Stripe, where she built the sales organization from its founding. Her litmus test for go-to-market team quality — that a room of ten engineers should require a minimum of ten minutes before identifying an account executive as not a product manager — is reproduced from the conservatory lecture as a standard the faculty has not improved upon. Her observation regarding customer buying motivation (eighty percent pain avoidance and risk reduction, twenty percent upside capture) is reproduced from the same session and has been confirmed independently in the faculty's own inquiry. The instruction to wait for a documentable ICP before hiring a first salesperson — and the specific one-million-dollar ARR threshold as the approximate point at which this condition can be assessed — represents the consensus of the conservatory's extended review of practitioner testimony on this question.

Primary source: J. DeWitt Grosser, "What world-class GTM looks like in 2026," Lenny's Podcast, 30 November 2025.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
Write your ideal customer profile in one sentence: industry, company size, buyer role, and the specific pain that drives the purchase decision. If the sentence requires more than forty words, the ICP has not yet been found; the act of writing is the exercise, not the completion of it.
Map your current sales process from first contact to signed contract. Identify the single stage at which the highest percentage of qualified prospects disengage. Address that stage — and only that stage — before any other optimization is attempted.
Ask five people at your company, from different functions, to describe your go-to-market motion in one sentence. The degree of divergence among the five answers is a direct measurement of the integration problem, if one exists. Do not explain the answers to one another before writing them down.

Étude X · Opus 1, No. 10 · Grade I (In Theory) / Grade VIII (In Practice)

Study for the Precipitous Left Hand

On the Error of Tempo and Its Consequences

Troppo presto, e poi silenzio
Too fast, and then silence
Grade I / Grade VIII

Dedicated to the Headcount That Was Added Before the Product Was Ready

The student is advised to resist filling this staff before PMF is established. Rachitsky is direct: growth has nothing to grow until it does.

The grade notation for this étude requires explanation. The technical concept it addresses is among the simplest in the conservatory's curriculum: do not accelerate before the foundation supports acceleration. A student of Grade I proficiency can understand this instruction. The difficulty — and the reason this étude warrants Grade VIII notation in its practical application — lies not in the comprehension of the rule but in the psychological conditions under which it must be applied. The student who has raised capital, who is facing a board, who is surrounded by competitors who appear to be moving faster, and who has been told repeatedly that speed is a virtue — this student will find the instruction to slow down nearly impossible to follow. The étude is easy. The conditions are not.

The phenomenon of premature scaling — spending significant money on growth before discovering and developing product-market fit — is among the most common causes of startup failure documented in the faculty's research. Andreessen Horowitz, citing the formulation of Steve Blank, has given it a name precise enough that the faculty reproduces it without modification. A business is scaling prematurely if it is spending significant amounts of money on growth before it has discovered and developed product-market fit. The student should note the word "significant." The indictment is not the expenditure of any resources before fit is found; it is the expenditure of resources at a scale that presupposes fit has been found when it has not. The distinction is the difference between exploring a route and paving it before it has been confirmed as correct.

The corrective technique is not, as some students conclude, to practice more slowly in all things. It is rather to identify precisely which activities require the foundation of product-market fit before they can produce their intended returns, and to sequence those activities accordingly. Hiring a head of sales before the sales motion is understood is an example of this error. Investing in paid acquisition before the retention curve has flattened is another. Expanding the product surface area before the core use case has proven itself is a third. Each of these errors shares a common structure: the cart is placed before the horse with great efficiency and at considerable expense. The horse, when it arrives, is confused. The cart has already been optimized for a different animal.

Raise capital when you are confident that you can use it to attain a higher growth rate and the higher growth rate is worth the trade-off in dilution. — Tomasz Tunguz, as reproduced in Lenny's Newsletter

The student who is offended by the implication that they are capable of understanding a Grade I concept but incapable of executing it is encouraged to examine the evidence.

Primary source: L. Rachitsky, "When should a SaaS startup raise money," Lenny's Newsletter, 25 February 2020; Steve Blank, "The Startup Owner's Manual," as cited in Andreessen Horowitz published materials, 2017.
Practice Assignment — Complete Before Proceeding
List every significant expenditure made in the last ninety days. For each, write one sentence explaining which evidence of product-market fit justified it.
Identify the earliest point at which your company began spending at a rate that presupposed fit. Assess honestly whether fit was present at that point.
For any hire made in the last six months, write the specific market signal that made that hire necessary. If you cannot write it, you have useful information.
